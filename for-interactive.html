The Premise

You are newly dead. The afterlife has a dress code.

Sinners in purgatory must sew garments for deities to work off their sins. When you arrive, flashbacks to your life start bleeding through — and suddenly the line between holy duty and human longing gets very complicated.

The afterlife is cosmic and hand-painted. The flashback scenes are drawn on actual notebook paper. The world looks different depending on whether you're human — and that difference is built into the art direction, not just the writing.

The Variables

Three tensions. Three endings.

Harmony Stability with the group
Angel Favor Goodwill with the system's enforcer

Some choices are marked in gold. They feel like shortcuts. They cost you in ways the interface doesn't announce.

The Endings

✊

Workers' Strike

Collective action prevails. The system cracks — briefly.

⚙️

Absorbed

You become what you arrived in. The boutique continues.

🧵

Endless Cycle

No resolution. The work goes on. The thread never ends.

The endings are not ranked. The game does not tell you which one is right.

The Design Thinking

The mechanic was built to put the player in the same position as the characters: aware that something is being measured, uncertain what the right answer is, complicit in the outcome regardless. The HUD is always visible. What the bars actually mean only becomes clear in retrospect.

This is what ludonarrative harmony looks like when the subject matter is labour, collective action, and complicity — the game's form and its politics have to agree with each other, or the whole thing collapses. Inspired by Sita Sings the Blues and Omori.

Project 02

Sort It Out

Mobile Web Game  ·  AR Prototype  ·  BSU Recycling Campaign  ·  2025

A mobile-first recycling game built as the interactive layer of a campus AR activation — flick items into the right bin, earn points, learn what actually gets recycled.

The Premise

The bin is no longer a passive receptacle.

Sort It Out is triggered by scanning a QR code placed directly on a campus recycling or trash bin — intercepting the student at the exact moment the disposal decision happens. The AR overlay makes recycling rules legible through consequence, not instruction.

No poster or video can replicate the experience of holding a greasy pizza box and having to consciously decide where it goes. The intervention is site-specific by design.

The Loop

Scan. Sort. Stamp. Repeat.

Scan a QR code → a location splash names the site ("You're at Woodworth — let's see what ends up here") → eight waste items appear as AR overlays → flick each toward the correct bin. Wrong sorts trigger a one-line explanation before the item resets. No item is simply marked wrong and discarded.

Finishing a location stamps it on a campus-wide collection map. Completing all locations earns a redeemable dining coupon.

The System

A campus-scale social layer.

Points are attributed to hall-based teams, with a live leaderboard surfaced in-game — "Your floor is currently ranked 4th on campus." Each location carries site-specific narrative text developed with the residence hall or department it serves, so the game at Woodworth is not the same game as the game at DeHority.

A node in a larger storyworld.

Sort It Out is not a standalone minigame — it is a spatial node in a documentary campaign following a recycled item from bin to processing facility. The documentary's narrative is the backstory that Sort It Out makes physically present at the moment of disposal. QR codes on bins are the physical touchpoints that connect the digital and real-world layers.
