▸ A NOTE ON METHOD

This case study is written as an autoethnography — a form of qualitative research where the researcher's own experience is simultaneously the subject and the method of inquiry. The design choices in Hanging by a Thread cannot be separated from the personal and political questions that generated them, so this document doesn't try.

🔍
Step 01 Empathy Research

What I was researching — and what I was really researching

The game started with a question I couldn't stop asking: what does it mean to comply with a system that was never built for you? The autoethnographic method felt right here because the research and the researcher are the same thing. I was not studying labour from a distance. I was thinking about the particular texture of compliance available to people who must participate in structures that diminish them and still call it gratitude.

I turned to labour theory — Silvia Federici's work on reproductive and care labour, bell hooks on the politics of margin and centre, the long tradition of South Asian subaltern thought on the relationship between work, caste, and spiritual obligation. I looked at mythological frameworks: Hindu cosmology has a deeply bureaucratic afterlife, full of divine administrators processing the moral ledgers of the dead. That combination — cosmic hierarchy and labour as sin-clearing — gave me a world that was literal enough to be comic and dense enough to carry weight.

I also researched games that had done something similar: Omori's dissociation mechanics, where the horror is embedded in the interface itself; Papers, Please's bureaucratic complicity by design; Sita Sings the Blues's formal playfulness about narrative authority and women's stories. The question I brought to these texts was always: can a game's form carry the politics of its content?

"The research question became: can a game make you participate in something you would otherwise critique from a distance?"

The autoethnographic element was this: I was also drawing on specific, non-theoretical experience — growing up Tamil and diasporic, watching community members navigate institutions that required them to be grateful for less, the peculiar emotional labour of performing contentment inside a system of managed scarcity. That experience is the texture beneath the worldbuilding. It doesn't appear in the game directly. It informs every design decision.

🎯
Step 02 Define

The design problem,
honestly stated

Most political games tell players what to think. The more interesting design problem is making the player enact something they would otherwise only understand abstractly. The challenge I set myself: make complicity felt, not just illustrated.

The purgatory setting solved multiple problems at once. It made labour and spiritual obligation literally synonymous. It allowed for a cosmic hierarchy that players would recognise as absurd — which is the correct emotional register for structural critique. And it gave me the flashback structure I needed: the human world bleeding into the afterlife, memory as disruption, the past making the present legible.

▸ DESIGN CONSTRAINT

The HUD must be visible at all times. The player must always be aware they are being measured. But what the bars actually represent should only become clear in retrospect — after choices have been made, after the ending has played, after it is too late to redo them differently.

This constraint came directly from the empathy research: the experience of being inside a system of measurement you didn't design and can't fully interpret is itself politically meaningful. The game needed to reproduce that experience structurally, not just describe it. The HUD as permanent, opaque presence was the formal solution.

The design question was then: what are the variables? What gets measured in a purgatory boutique? And what happens to each of the three tensions when you maximise one at the expense of the others?

Step 03 Ideate

Building purgatory:
the world, the characters, the choices

The boutique emerged as the central space because of its dual valence: commerce and aesthetics, labour and beauty, compliance and craft. A place where beautiful things are made under conditions that are not beautiful — which is a description of most of the fashion industry and also of a great deal of creative labour. The setting is cosmic in its design and domestic in its stakes.

The character roster grew from that centre. The Angel is not a villain; she is middle management — the enforcer of a system she is also inside, doing her job with the conviction that the system works. Raji, the senior worker, has found a way to survive that the player will initially read as wisdom and eventually understand as accommodation. You are newly arrived, and the relationship between those two figures is what you are navigating.

The visual design carries the politics too. The afterlife scenes are hand-painted and cosmic — saturated, formal, enormous in scale. The flashback scenes are drawn on actual notebook paper, scanned. The human world looks handmade in ways the divine world does not. This is not an arbitrary aesthetic choice: it is a statement about where intimacy and imperfection are permitted to exist.

The three-variable system was designed so that maximising any one value costs you in the others. High Harmony means you are part of the collective but possibly at the cost of individual advancement. High Angel Favor means the system's enforcer likes you, which comes at a social cost. High Raji alignment means following a path of survival that has its own moral logic — but it is not resistance. There is no "good" playthrough. There are only honest ones.

"Some choices are marked in gold. They feel like shortcuts. They cost you in ways the interface doesn't announce."

🛠
Step 04 Prototype

Building in
the dark

I built in Ren'Py, a visual novel engine with a Python backbone. Its logic — scenes, labels, jumps, flags — maps well onto linear branching narrative but requires careful architecture to support more complex state management. The HUD required custom screens and persistent variable tracking across scenes. The branching script carries multiple paths toward three distinct endings.

The notebook-paper flashback scenes were drawn by hand on actual paper, photographed, and composited into the Ren'Py environment. They function as scene transitions and as evidence: the human world is not merely remembered, it is felt as a different material reality than the afterlife. The paper has texture. The ink has weight. The scenes have a presence the cosmic sequences do not.

The art direction is still in progress — character redraw with an external collaborator is underway. The current prototype represents the full game structure: world, characters, branching script, HUD system, and endings. What changes in revision is the visual polish of the character work, not the architecture of the experience.

Step 05 Test

What players
actually did

Playtesting happened in EMDD 630 with peers in the interactive storytelling programme. The primary finding: most playtesters did not understand what the HUD bars were tracking until the second or third playthrough — which was by design. The HUD being opaque on first pass reproduces the experience the game is about.

The more interesting finding: players felt guilty about choices they had consciously made. Not because they were told to — because the formal structure of a branching narrative, where each choice is visible and deliberate, makes complicity impossible to attribute to chance. You chose. The game records it. The bar moved. This is what ludonarrative harmony looks like when the subject is labour and moral responsibility.

🧵

Endless
Cycle

No resolution. The work goes on. The thread never ends. The most unsettling — players recognised themselves.

"The endings are not ranked. The game does not tell you which one is right. That absence is the point."

The next iteration addresses art direction (character redraw), script revision based on playtester feedback about pacing in act two, and the introduction of a glossary system for players unfamiliar with the mythological references. The core mechanic is not under revision. It worked.
