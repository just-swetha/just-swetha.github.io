Step 01 Empathy Research

The disposal decision:
primary and secondary research

This project builds on primary research conducted in EMDD 620 Design Thinking (Fall 2025) by Ava, Femi, and Selasi, who first identified contamination patterns at Ball State waste stations. The EMDD 640 team extended that work through new observation, interviews, and journey mapping, while situating the findings in a broader secondary literature on campus recycling behaviour.

Ball State's recycling program — managed in partnership with Republic Services and the Muncie Sanitary District — accepts paper, cardboard, glass, metals, and specific plastics collected in blue bags. Despite this infrastructure, empathy research consistently found the same pattern: students slowed down at waste stations but rarely read signage. The physical act of sorting was happening in a near-total information vacuum.

▸ PRIMARY · OBSERVATION

Waste Station Watch

Observation at dining and residence hall stations (Woodworth, DeHority, Student Center). Tracking pause behaviour, contamination sources, and moments of visible uncertainty. Food-soiled paper — especially greasy pizza boxes — emerged as the single most common contamination source.

▸ PRIMARY · INTERVIEWS

Facilities + Sustainability Staff

Interviews with Sustainability Officers and Republic Services staff revealed that contamination rates were high enough to divert entire loads to landfill. The Muncie facility enforces a strict 10% contamination threshold; loads above it are rejected entirely.

▸ PRIMARY · JOURNEY MAPPING

Student Disposal Journey

Collaborative journey mapping with ~10 students representing diverse majors and recycling familiarity. Sessions mapped where confusion arises — particularly in rushed between-class moments when the disposal decision is made in under five seconds.

▸ PRIMARY · DISCOURSE ANALYSIS

Online & Student Media

Ball Bearings and Ball State Daily News coverage (Liakas, 2025) and Reddit discourse reflect widespread student frustration: unclear rules, inconsistent bin placement, and scepticism that recycling is actually processed. This is the "value-action gap" in lived form.

▸ SECONDARY RESEARCH

Behaviour Gap Literature

Noh (2021), Cho (2019), and Yusoff et al. (2024) confirm that recycling participation is driven by perceived behavioural control and clear guidance — not general awareness. The gap between intention and action is widest at the point of disposal.

▸ SECONDARY RESEARCH

National + Regional Context

The EPA (2023) documents that contamination thresholds as low as 10% cause entire loads to be landfilled. Midwest recycling rates lag behind the Northeast and Pacific Coast (National Academies, 2025) — meaning BSU students may have had less prior exposure to strict recycling culture.

"Students broadly knew recycling mattered. They did not know, at the moment of holding something over a bin, what Ball State's specific rules were — and existing infrastructure wasn't telling them."

The synthesis from research: recycling at Ball State is not a logistical problem or an awareness problem. It is a narrative coherence problem. The institutional story of sustainability (geothermal energy, climate action plans, blue-bag programs) does not connect, at the moment of disposal, to the student standing over a bin with a greasy cup. The storyworld fractures precisely where it needs to hold.

Step 02 Define

Not an awareness problem.
A narrative coherence problem.

The problem statement that emerged: Ball State operates as a narrative ecosystem. Institutional messaging (sustainability website, orientation, email campaigns, signage) constructs a story of collective environmental progress. Informal narratives circulate through student journalism, Reddit threads, and peer conversations — where recycling is frequently described as confusing, inconvenient, and possibly symbolic. These parallel narratives coexist but do not align. When coherence weakens across platforms, behaviour weakens with it.

The existing tools operate at the wrong moment. Posters and orientation modules build awareness weeks before the disposal decision happens. They cannot intervene at the bin.

▸ HOW MIGHT WE

How might we design a transmedia storytelling experience that uses narrative, interaction, and participation to reshape how people understand, feel about, and engage with recycling — meeting students at the moment the disposal decision happens?

▸ DESIGN PARAMETERS

Site-specific by design: The game at Woodworth must not be the same game as the game at DeHority. Each location carries narrative text developed with the community that uses that space.

Mobile-first: Students are on their phones at the bin. The intervention meets them there. No app download required — QR code triggers a mobile browser experience.

Consequence-based, not instructional: A wrong sort triggers explanation before the item resets. No item is simply marked wrong and discarded. Every error is a teaching moment.

Embedded in a larger story: A standalone minigame has no reason to return to. Sort It Out is one spatial node in a transmedia documentary campaign — the physical touchpoint that connects digital and real-world layers.

Measurable target: A 20% improvement in correct recycling behaviour in two dining halls by end of Spring 2027 deployment.

💡
Step 03 Ideate

Sort It Out as one node
in a larger storyworld

The design team developed a transmedia storyworld structured as a Freytag arc: from exposition (students encounter basic information about the recycling system) through rising action (students discover the scale of the contamination problem through interconnected content) to climax (collective confrontation, coordinated participation, visible outcomes) to resolution (recycling reframed as a permanent part of Ball State community identity).

The storyworld contains five interconnected story nodes. Sort It Out is the physical, spatial node — the only one that can intercept the student at the exact moment the disposal decision happens.

Leaderboard

Sort It Out's specific contribution to the storyworld: it is the only node that operates spatially, at the physical bin, at the moment the behaviour it is trying to change actually occurs. No video, poster, or social post can replicate the experience of holding a greasy pizza box and having to consciously decide where it goes.

The game loop was designed around three motivational layers operating simultaneously: immediate consequence-based feedback (sort correctly → points; sort incorrectly → one-line explanation + reset), social competition (team points attributed by residence hall, live leaderboard: "Your floor is currently ranked 4th on campus"), and collection (campus-wide stamp map driving students to visit multiple locations).

01
Scan QR code on bin

Location splash appears: "You're at Woodworth — let's see what ends up here." Site-specific narrative text, developed in collaboration with each residence hall or department, greets the player from the first second.

02
Sort eight waste items

Items appear as AR overlays in the camera view. Flick each toward the correct bin. Wrong sorts trigger a one-line explanation ("Greasy cardboard contaminates the whole batch — always trash") before the item resets. No item is discarded without teaching first.

03
Stamp the location, climb the leaderboard

Completing a location stamps it on the campus-wide collection map. Your floor's ranking updates. The map makes all QR locations visible, drawing students to explore campus.

04
Complete all locations, earn a reward

Completing all campus locations unlocks a digital dining coupon, negotiated as a campaign partnership — a reward designed to motivate the Indifferent student who won't move for environmental conviction alone.

"Sort It Out is not a standalone minigame. It is the spatial node in a transmedia documentary campaign — the physical touchpoint that makes the documentary's story present at the moment of disposal."

The non-standardisation across locations is a deliberate design choice: the game at Woodworth is not the same game as the game at DeHority, because those communities have different waste streams and different stories. This site-specificity is what makes a student who plays at Woodworth want to also scan the bin at the Student Center. The novelty is structural, not cosmetic.

Step 04 Prototype

The mechanic,
demonstrated

The web-based prototype demonstrates the core sort-and-explain mechanic: waste items appear, the player sorts them by flicking toward the correct bin, wrong sorts trigger one-line explanations before resetting, correct sorts score points. Built mobile-first in HTML, CSS, and JavaScript, playable in-browser on any smartphone without a download. The prototype was coded with the assistance of Claude (Anthropic).

▸ WHAT THIS PROTOTYPE DEMONSTRATES

The sort-and-explain loop is satisfying and teachable. The explanation model — brief, specific, consequence-framed — works better than signage as a teaching mechanism at the point of disposal.

Full AR implementation (camera overlay, geofencing, QR trigger, live leaderboard backend, location-specific item sets) is outside the scope of this prototype. This is a proof of concept for the interactive layer, not the finished product.

Step 05 Test

Formative testing,
summative goals

Testing is structured in two phases aligned with the production and deployment timeline.

▸ FORMATIVE TESTING (Fall 2025 → Spring 2026 prototyping)

Think Aloud Protocol: Task-based walkthroughs with 5–8 students per round. Participants narrate everything they notice, question, or feel while using the game. The facilitator listens without guiding. The data comes from what students actually do, not what they report they would do. Think Aloud reveals friction at the bin — moments where the spatial overlay feels disorienting, where explanation text moves too fast, where a wrong-sort explanation doesn't land.

Concept testing questions (after each session): Does this make sense to you? What would you change? Does this feel relevant to your daily life?

Early social media analytics: Engagement data from Explainer Series and Comedic Reels posts (saves, DM shares, comments) as leading indicators of content resonance before full production begins. DM shares are the strongest signal of non-follower reach.

▸ PROTOTYPE FINDING

The explanation model works

Wrong-sort explanations were rated more immediately useful than signage. Players reported learning specific rules they hadn't known — especially the pizza box and plastic bag rules.

▸ PROTOTYPE FINDING

Site-specificity resonates

Location-specific splash screens landed well. Players responded to seeing their hall named — the game felt locally relevant rather than generic, which is the entire design premise.

▸ STILL TO TEST

Live leaderboard motivation

Without a functional leaderboard backend in the prototype, the social competition layer is untested. "Your floor is 4th on campus" needs real data to be felt — not to be simulated.

▸ STILL TO TEST

QR trigger in situ

The prototype cannot test the QR-triggered launch at a physical bin. The critical question — does intercepting the student at the disposal moment change the behaviour? — requires real-world deployment to answer.

▸ SUMMATIVE TESTING (Spring 2027 deployment)

Pre/post survey design: Administered at move-in (baseline) and before finals (post-campaign). Measures recycling confidence, correct item identification, and self-reported disposal habits. Modelled on Digital Literacy Assessment Survey methodology.

Physical contamination data: Contamination rate data from Facilities Planning & Management and Republic Services, compared against Fall 2026 baseline. Primary goal: 20% improvement in correct recycling in two target dining halls.

App analytics: Sorting challenge completions, contamination score changes over the semester, residence hall performance comparison across the leaderboard.

Semi-structured exit interviews: 10–15 students including both engaged participants and passive-exposure-only students. Capturing which story elements were most memorable, most useful, and most likely to have changed behaviour at the bin.

"The test is not whether players found the game fun. The test is whether, the next time they stand at a bin holding a greasy cup, they know what to do."

The project is not complete. The prototype demonstrates that the mechanic works and the framework is sound. What the testing phase will verify is whether that mechanic — embedded in the full storyworld, triggered at the physical bin, connected to a live leaderboard and a documentary campaign — produces a measurable change in the behaviour it was designed to change.
